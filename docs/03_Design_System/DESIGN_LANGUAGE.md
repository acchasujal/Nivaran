# CivicPulse: Design Language (CDL)

This document defines the brand identity, voice guidelines, design principles, and microcopy tone rules for CivicPulse.

---

## 1. Design Manifesto

CivicPulse is a witness, not a megaphone.

It does not shout on the citizen's behalf. It does not perform outrage or manufacture urgency. It stands next to a person's evidence and says, plainly: *here is what this is, here is what happened to it, here is what remains true and what remains uncertain.*

A civic platform earns trust the way a courtroom clerk earns trust — not by being persuasive, but by being exact, unhurried, and impossible to catch in an overstatement. Every design decision in this language optimizes for that kind of trust: the trust of a records office, not the trust of a campaign poster.

---

## 2. Brand Identity & Personality

**Visual Register:** The competent clerk. An organized public records office or a well-run public library. GOV.UK's calm authority, crossed with the quiet confidence of a well-built financial ledger.

**What we are NOT:** A startup dashboard trying to look impressive. A gamified consumer app. A social feed optimizing for outrage or engagement.

### Core Adjectives
*   **Exact:** Evidence-first products live or die on precision. Reject vague status labels like "processing...".
*   **Unhurried:** Trust is built slowly. Reject countdown timers and artificial urgency banners.
*   **Legible:** Clear translation of complex legal and technical concepts.
*   **Quietly Indian:** Rooted in the country's actual civic and infrastructural landscape, not global SaaS clichés.
*   **Accountable:** Every screen identifies who owns the next action.

---

## 3. Design Principles

1.  **Evidence outranks assertion:** Never display a claim with more visual confidence than its proof justifies.
2.  **Uncertainty is shown, not hidden:** Pending statuses and AI-classification caveats are first-class visual citizens.
3.  **Calm over urgent:** Lower the user's heart rate, don't raise it.
4.  **Plain language by default:** Place technical and legal precision one tap away, never in the first sentence.
5.  **Every state has an owner:** A status never floats without identifying the responsible party.
6.  **Progressive disclosure:** Keep screens simple. Provide deep details on request.
7.  **One primary action per screen:** Direct the user's eye to exactly one next step.
8.  **Consistency is accessibility:** Keep color codes, card shapes, and interactions uniform.
9.  **Design for low-bandwidth mobile first:** The desktop judge experience is downstream of this.
10. **Nothing is decorative:** Every color, layout spacing, and visual element must encode meaning.

---

## 4. Microcopy & Tone Guide

Our tone is the **calm clerk**. Confirm plainly, never celebrate loudly, and explain errors constructively.

| Context | Do | Don't |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Success** | "Your evidence joined 4 other reports in this area." | "🎉 Your Report Mattered!" |
| **Error** | "We couldn't read this photo. Try a clearer, well-lit shot of the issue." | "Upload failed. Error 400." |
| **Warning** | "This match isn't certain yet — we've kept your report separate to be safe." | "Low confidence: 0.47" |
| **AI explanation** | "Our system thinks this is a pothole, based on the photo. A human hasn't confirmed this yet." | "Agent 1: Classification complete. Confidence: 0.91" |
| **Privacy** | "Only your ward and the reviewing officer can see your exact location." | *(any privacy claim left unstated)* |
| **Government** | "Sent to the Ward Office on 14 July. We'll update you when they respond." | "202 Accepted" |

**Tone Consistency Rule:** The register must remain identical across the intake wizard, error messages, and legal draft viewers. Avoid shifting between "developer console" and "government bureaucracy" tones.
